Jean Watson

"Theory of Human Caring"
Some may be concerned that as modern medicine progresses, healthcare
may become more analytical and less altruistic.
American nurse and theorist Jean Watson created the
Theory of Human Caring in response to this concern.
According to Watson's method, caring and curing are two different things.
According to her, the term "curing" encompasses not only
the physical recovery from an illness
but also the mental and spiritual aspects of healing.
Nursing should take into account "carative" aspects in addition to curative ones,
or those that deal with a patient's physical problems.
The guidelines that contemporary nurses
can utilize to practice healing of the mind, body,
and spirit are provided by carative elements.
These elements show the difference between nursing and medicine
and offer the structural foundation for Watson's Theory of Caring.




Watson's definition of health is said to involve far more than just treating a disease.
She thinks that while healing is essential, it goes well beyond a body's reaction.
Nurses must treat patients holistically—that is, with an emphasis on the mind, body, and soul—in order to promote wellness.
According to her, a person's bodily, mental, and spiritual selves must all be in perfect balance for healing to take place.



Watson firmly believes that the nursing process is greatly influenced by the environment.
Nurses might be impacted by their surroundings in addition to patients and how well they recuperate.
One instance given included a nursing unit that had an unprecedented drop in morale.
Due to this the nursing management came up with the notion to implement some of Watson's philosophies on the unit as a result,
Staff members found this to be quite helpful, and in the end, this strengthened the relationships and
morale within the unit among coworkers (Summerell, 2015).



Watson advances the profession by her interpretation of the nursing metaparadigm, person.
of providing for human needs via human care. Regardless of a person's desires, cultures, or beliefs,
she feels that each person has a distinct and unpredictable set of requirements that must be accepted and honored.
She also believes that a holistic approach that addresses the mind, body, and soul is essential for a person
to receive full healing.
